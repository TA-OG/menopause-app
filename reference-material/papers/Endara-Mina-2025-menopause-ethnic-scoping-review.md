## Methods
- Joanna Briggs Institute scoping review methodology
- PRISMA-ScR reporting
- 7 databases: Medline/PubMed, Web of Science, Scopus
- No language or time restrictions
- 446 initial records → 374 eligible → 63 full texts → 20 met inclusion criteria

## Key definition — Ethnicity
"A social group whose members share a sense of collective identity grounded in a common — whether real or perceived — ancestry, and expressed through shared cultural elements such as language, religion, values, practices, or a common history."
NOTE: Ethnicity in this review = self-identification. Acknowledges relational, socially constructed nature.

## KEY FINDINGS

### African American / Hispanic women:
- Greater emotional AND vasomotor symptom burden than white women
- Higher rates of psychological distress around menopause

### Asian women (Chinese, Japanese):
- Lowest prevalence of distress
- Frame menopause as a natural, expected life transition
- Less likely to seek medical intervention

### Indigenous women (varied):
- Māori women (New Zealand): frame menopause as continuation of life cycle, not pathology
- Indigenous Bolivian women: associated with 'loss of blood' and diminished energy but also release from reproductive constraints
- NOT uniformly negative — significant variation by community

### Migration and acculturation effects (CRITICAL FOR AUNTY MEL):
- Chinese migrant women in Europe increasingly adopted biomedical explanations
- Hispanic women in USA associated symptoms with stress, hormonal imbalance, aging
- North American women tended toward medicalised framing requiring clinical validation
- CONCLUSION: Where a woman lives significantly modifies how she experiences and reports symptoms

### Language as cultural marker:
- Some communities have no word for menopause
- Arab world: term means "desperate age" — pessimistic framing built into language
- Western medical language: "reproductive failure", "ovarian failure" — pathologising
- Māori, Indigenous: natural life cycle language

### Silence as cultural mechanism:
- African American women: silence as defence against healthcare racism
- Asian cultures: discussing menopause seen as "shameful" and "embarrassing"
- Both lead to under-seeking of care
- Silence transmitted intergenerationally

## CONCLUSIONS
1. Menopause is not merely biological — it is culturally embedded
2. Substantial ethnic differences in perception, reporting, and meaning of symptoms
3. Most research remains Western-centric and inadequate for non-Caucasian populations
4. Sub-ethnic variation (within broad categories) is under-researched but significant
5. Location (country of residence) modifies the menopause experience significantly

## RESEARCH GAP (relevant to Aunty Mel)
No existing studies compare symptom experience at sub-ethnic level (e.g., Yoruba vs Igbo, Tamil vs Punjabi, Cantonese vs Mandarin). Allan's critique of continent-level categorisation is validated by this literature gap.
